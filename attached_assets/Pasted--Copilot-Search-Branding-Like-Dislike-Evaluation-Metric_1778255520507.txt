Evaluation Metrics for ML Models in Healthcare
Evaluating machine learning models in healthcare requires both statistical performance metrics and clinical interpretability because decisions can have life-altering consequences.

Core Classification Metrics
For binary classification (e.g., disease vs. no disease), common metrics include GeeksForGeeks+1:

Accuracy: Proportion of correct predictions. Useful overall but misleading in imbalanced datasets (e.g., rare disease detection).

Precision: Proportion of true positives among all positive predictions. Important when false positives are costly.

Recall (Sensitivity): Proportion of true positives among all actual positives. Critical when missing a diagnosis is dangerous.

F1 Score: Harmonic mean of precision and recall, balancing both.

Specificity: Proportion of true negatives among all actual negatives. High specificity reduces unnecessary follow-ups.

Positive Predictive Value (PPV): Probability that a positive prediction is correct. Clinically relevant for triage decisions.

Area Under the Curve (AUC-ROC)
AUC-ROC measures the model’s ability to distinguish between classes across all possible thresholds. High AUC (>0.9) suggests strong discrimination, but it can mask poor performance in subgroups moldstud.com.

Calibration and Threshold Analysis
Calibration plots check if predicted probabilities match actual outcomes, ensuring reliable risk estimates moldstud.com.

Threshold optimization adjusts decision boundaries to balance sensitivity and specificity for clinical needs.

Special Considerations in Healthcare
Imbalanced datasets: Rare events (e.g., cancer) require metrics beyond accuracy; focus on recall, precision, and F1.

False positives vs. false negatives: In diagnostics, false negatives are often more critical; in screening, false positives can cause alarm fatigue.

Subgroup analysis: Models may perform well overall but fail in specific patient groups; validate across demographics moldstud.com.

Interpretability: Use SHAP, LIME, or other explainability tools to build clinician trust moldstud.com.

Generalization and Robustness
Cross-validation and hold-out test sets ensure the model generalizes to unseen patients imipark.github.io.

Monitor for overfitting (high training accuracy, low validation accuracy) and underfitting (poor performance on both sets) imipark.github.io.

Practical Example
A gastroenterology ML model detecting colon polyps might report:

Sensitivity 95% (catches most polyps)

Specificity 60% (flags many false positives)

PPV 20% (only 1 in 5 alerts is true)

AUC 0.92 (strong discrimination)

Calibration plot showing good probability alignment

Interpretation: The model is good at detecting polyps but generates many false alarms, so clinicians may need to refine thresholds or combine with expert review.

In summary: In healthcare ML, combine standard ML metrics with clinical context, calibration checks, and interpretability to ensure models are not only statistically sound but also clinically actionable Nature+2.
